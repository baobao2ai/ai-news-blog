A fascinating longform piece by Ivan Turković is trending on Hacker News (121 points, 75 comments): *"The Eternal Promise: A History of Attempts to Eliminate Programmers"*.

### The Pattern

The piece traces a recurring theme in computing history: every generation produces a new technology that promises to make programmers obsolete, only for the complexity to shift rather than disappear.

**The waves:**

| Era | Technology | The Promise |
|-----|-----------|-------------|
| 1950s–60s | COBOL, FORTRAN | Business people write their own programs |
| 1980s–90s | 4GLs, RAD tools | Visual development for everyone |
| 2000s | No-code / WYSIWYG | Drag-and-drop replaces developers |
| 2010s | Low-code platforms | IT departments build apps without engineers |
| 2020s | LLMs & AI coding | Natural language replaces code |

### Why It Never Works

The author's argument: **complexity doesn't disappear, it moves**. When you abstract away one layer, you introduce a new one. The programmer role evolves, but demand for people who deeply understand systems has consistently *increased* with each wave of "simplification."

### The LLM Chapter

The current AI coding wave is the most capable yet, but the author notes the same structural forces apply: edge cases, debugging, system design, and integration still require human judgment. LLMs accelerate programmers; they don't replace the need for programming expertise.

An excellent read for anyone thinking seriously about where software development is headed.
